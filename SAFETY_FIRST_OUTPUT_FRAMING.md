# Safety-First Output Framing & User Language
## Passagr Result Card Framework

---

## Part 1: Reusable Result Card Template

### Result Card Structure

Each result returned to a user is structured as a single "safety card" containing four mandatory sections. The card is presented without ranking, marketing language, or comparative framing—only factual statement of trade-offs.

---

### **SECTION A: Why This Option Is Safer Than The U.S.**
**Purpose:** State the concrete legal protection that exists in the destination.
**Rules:**
- Cite the specific law or statute (e.g., "Hate crime law includes sexual orientation" vs. "safe country")
- Reference Agent 1 Safety Baseline data (LGBTQ+ rights index, abortion status, hate crime protections)
- Include the data confidence level and date of last update (e.g., "current as of January 2026")
- Do NOT claim the protection will solve the user's specific problem
- Do NOT compare emotional safety or cultural acceptance (only legal protections)

**Template Language:**
```
[COUNTRY] has [SPECIFIC LEGAL PROTECTION] as of [DATE].

Definition: [What the law protects].

Source: [Government/NGO source cited in research].

This differs from U.S. law because [SPECIFIC DIFFERENCE].
Current status: [High confidence / Moderate confidence / Conditional - flag pending legislation].
```

**Example of What NOT to Write:**
- ❌ "You'll be safe in Portugal because it's so welcoming."
- ❌ "Spain offers the best protection for LGBTQ+ people."
- ❌ "Germany respects your rights."

**Example of What TO Write:**
- ✅ "Portugal classifies hate crimes based on sexual orientation under Law 5/99. Conviction carries sentences of up to 5 years imprisonment. The U.S. has no federal hate crime law addressing sexual orientation (only some states)."

---

### **SECTION B: What Makes It Difficult**
**Purpose:** Name the specific barriers tied to Agent 2's intake questions.
**Rules:**
- State ONLY the barriers relevant to the user's intake answers (don't list all possible barriers)
- Use the language of administrative requirement, not moral judgment
- Cite processing timeline, documentation needs, or procedural complexity from research
- Do NOT soften the language or suggest workarounds not in the research

**Template Language:**
```
The [PATHWAY TYPE] pathway requires:

1. [REQUIREMENT] - must [SPECIFIC ACTION]
   *Difficulty:* [Why this is hard, tied to intake question].
   *Documentation/Timeline:* [What Passagr research shows].

2. [REQUIREMENT] - must [SPECIFIC ACTION]
   *Difficulty:* [Why this is hard, tied to intake question].
   *Documentation/Timeline:* [What Passagr research shows].

Note: Users who answered [INTAKE QUESTION] differently may face different barriers.
```

**Example of What NOT to Write:**
- ❌ "It's hard but you can definitely do this."
- ❌ "The documentation is just routine paperwork."
- ❌ "Most people get through this faster than you'd think."

**Example of What TO Write:**
- ✅ "The EU Asylum Procedure requires physical presence in the destination country to lodge the initial claim. If you cannot travel to Germany safely or legally, you cannot file an asylum claim from the U.S. The research shows this is a documented first-step requirement; no offshore application pathway exists under German law."

---

### **SECTION C: The Fastest Viable Legal Pathway**
**Purpose:** Describe the quickest route with realistic timeline and what "viable" means.
**Rules:**
- List steps in sequence, not as a checklist
- Include processing timeline from research (e.g., "Canada Express Entry: 10–14 months current backlog")
- State what "viable" means: includes acceptance likelihood, documentation requirements, and any conditional flags
- Do NOT imply this is the recommended pathway (only the fastest)
- Flag any current backlogs or changes noted in research (current as of January 2026)

**Template Language:**
```
**Fastest pathway:** [PATHWAY NAME]
**Timeline:** [START DATE] → [POINT 1] (X weeks/months) → [POINT 2] (Y weeks/months) → [OUTCOME]
**Current Status:** [BACKLOG FLAG / STANDARD PROCESSING / FAST-TRACK]

Step 1: [ACTION] — [TIMELINE]
Step 2: [ACTION] — [TIMELINE]
Step 3: [ACTION] — [TIMELINE]

What "viable" means for this pathway:
- You must [REQUIREMENT 1]
- You must [REQUIREMENT 2]
- The government agency will assess [CRITERIA] using standards from [SOURCE]

**Current challenge:** [Any backlogs, legislative changes, or procedural shifts noted in January 2026 research]
```

**Example of What NOT to Write:**
- ❌ "Spain's digital nomad visa is a breeze—just 3 weeks!"
- ❌ "You'll be approved quickly if you have the income."
- ❌ "This is the best and fastest option for you."

**Example of What TO Write:**
- ✅ "Fastest pathway: Spain Digital Nomad Visa.
Timeline: Application submission → Consular review (20 business days) → Approval & residence card issuance.
Current Status: Standard processing (no current backlog as of January 2026).
Step 1: Prepare documentation (proof of remote employment or client contracts, €2,762/month net income evidence). Step 2: Submit at Spanish consulate in your jurisdiction. Step 3: Attend consular interview if requested (varies by location). After approval: Register with Spanish municipal authorities within 30 days.
What 'viable' means: Spain requires proof of stable remote income at €2,762/month net. Income is verified against 3-6 months of bank statements. The visa lasts 1 year, renewable annually, and leads to permanent residence after 5 years of continuous legal residence."

---

### **SECTION D: A Fallback If The Primary Path Fails**
**Purpose:** Name ONE alternate pathway that works if the primary path becomes unavailable.
**Rules:**
- Choose a fallback that requires fundamentally different preconditions (not just a slower version of the same path)
- State clearly what would trigger the fallback (e.g., "if you cannot secure a job offer")
- Include timeline and barriers for the fallback
- Do NOT suggest the fallback is easier or more likely (only different)
- Keep it to one fallback only; do not list all alternatives

**Template Language:**
```
**If [PRIMARY PATH] is not viable:**

Alternative pathway: [FALLBACK PATHWAY]
**Triggers:** This applies if you [SPECIFIC CONDITION that makes primary path impossible].

Step 1: [ACTION] — [TIMELINE]
Step 2: [ACTION] — [TIMELINE]

What changes from the primary path:
- You no longer need [PRIMARY REQUIREMENT]
- You instead need [FALLBACK REQUIREMENT]
- Timeline shifts to [NEW TIMELINE]

**Additional barrier:** [Any new difficulty introduced by this pathway]

When to consider this fallback:
- If [TRIGGER 1], the primary path is eliminated
- If [TRIGGER 2], evaluate this alternative
- Talk to an immigration lawyer if [EDGE CASE]
```

**Example of What NOT to Write:**
- ❌ "Don't worry—there's always another option!"
- ❌ "You can just try the other pathway if the first one doesn't work out."
- ❌ "The backup plan is almost as good."

**Example of What TO Write:**
- ✅ "If the Portugal D7 Passive Income Visa cannot be pursued (e.g., you don't have €920/month in stable income), consider: Alternative pathway: Portugal D6 Temporary Residence Visa (works for persons of independent means with lower documentation but same income requirement).
Triggers: This applies if you have income but lack 1+ years of tax return documentation required for D7.
Step 1: Gather 3-6 months recent bank statements showing €920/month average. Step 2: Submit D6 application (30–60 days processing). Step 3: After 1 year on D6, convert to D7 once you meet documentation requirements.
What changes: D6 is renewable annually; D7 leads to permanent residency. Both require the same income threshold but D6 has faster initial approval.
Additional barrier: D6 does not lead to permanent residency on its own; you must transition to D7 after 1 year, requiring additional documentation."

## Part 2: Example Outputs

### **Example 1: "Safe But Slow" Output**
**Scenario:** User in the U.S. seeking to relocate for reproductive freedom; has documented European ancestry; can physically travel; has legal representation available.

**Country/Pathway:** Portugal via D7 Passive Income Visa + EU Asylum as fallback

---

#### **SECTION A: Why This Option Is Safer Than The U.S.**

Portugal has constitutional protections for reproductive freedom and hate crime laws addressing sexual orientation and gender identity.

**Definition:**
- Abortion is legal on request up to 10 weeks of pregnancy (70 days), extendable to 14 weeks (98 days) in cases of fetal abnormality or health risk.
- Hate crime law (Law 5/99) explicitly covers crimes motivated by sexual orientation, gender identity, or race. Conviction carries sentences of 1–5 years imprisonment depending on severity.
- EU Charter of Fundamental Rights prohibits discrimination based on sex (Article 21), including reproductive choice and gender identity.

**Source:** Portuguese law; ILGA-Europe Rainbow Map 2025; Center for Reproductive Rights World Abortion Laws database.

**This differs from U.S. law because:** The U.S. has no federal abortion protection (varies by state post-2022). No federal hate crime law addressing sexual orientation or gender identity exists; only select states have such protections.

**Current status:** High confidence. Portuguese law has been stable since 2007. EU protections reaffirmed in 2023.

---

#### **SECTION B: What Makes It Difficult**

The D7 Passive Income Visa requires:

1. **Proof of stable monthly income** — must show €920/month (~$950 USD) from a passive source (pensions, investments, rental income, dividends).
   *Difficulty:* If your primary income is active employment, you need to establish a secondary passive income stream or transfer savings to meet threshold.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* Requires 1–2 years of tax returns, recent bank statements, or pension documentation. If you lack 1+ year of tax history, Portugal offers the faster D6 Temporary Residence (see Fallback).

2. **Proof of legal residence in Portugal** — you must be physically present to apply in Portugal or apply at a Portuguese consulate in your country of residence.
   *Difficulty:* If you cannot travel safely or cannot take time away from your current location without alerting authorities or risking your situation, this pathway is unavailable.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* Consular processing takes 6+ months from application to residence card issuance.

3. **Medical screening and background check** — Portugal requires a tuberculosis test and certificate of no criminal record.
   *Difficulty:* If criminal records exist (even for charges unrelated to safety-seeking), approval may be delayed or denied.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* TB screening takes 1–2 weeks; background check verification from U.S. authorities takes 4–8 weeks.

**Note:** Users who answered "No" to Intake Question 1 (physical presence) cannot pursue this pathway. Users without stable monthly income below €920 should explore the D6 fallback or employment pathways instead.

---

#### **SECTION C: The Fastest Viable Legal Pathway**

**Fastest pathway:** Portugal D7 Passive Income Visa
**Timeline:** Month 1 (documentation preparation) → Month 2–4 (consular processing) → Month 5–7 (residence card issuance & in-country registration) → Month 6–12 (settlement).
**Current Status:** Standard processing (no current backlog as of January 2026).

**Step 1: Document your passive income** (1–2 months)
- Gather 1–2 years of tax returns, recent bank statements (6+ months), pension statements, or investment account statements showing €920/month average.
- If income is from rental property, provide lease agreement and proof of deposits.
- If income is from investments, provide brokerage statements.

**Step 2: Submit application at Portuguese consulate** (1 month)
- Complete application form (online via consulate or by mail).
- Submit: passport, income documentation, medical certificate (TB test), criminal background check from U.S. authorities, proof of accommodation in Portugal (rental agreement or property deed), travel insurance, and health insurance documentation.

**Step 3: Attend consular interview** (if requested; 1–2 weeks)
- Consulates may request clarification on income sources or employment history.
- Interview conducted in English if you request interpreter; note this adds 1–2 weeks.

**Step 4: Receive residence card & register in Portugal** (6–8 weeks after approval)
- Once approved, travel to Portugal and register with local municipal authority (Junta de Freguesia) within 30 days.
- Register with immigration authority (SEF) to receive residence card (D7 Residence Card).

**What "viable" means for this pathway:**
- You must have access to €920/month from a source classified as passive income (not primary employment).
- You must be able to travel to Portugal or a Portuguese consulate to initiate the process.
- You must pass a background check in the U.S. (criminal record may delay or disqualify).
- You must commit to residing in Portugal; the visa is checked annually for proof of residence.

**Current challenge:** None. D7 processing is routine. However, income verification can be delayed if your bank is slow to provide statements or if you have irregular passive income (e.g., dividends that vary monthly). Plan 2–3 months for documentation alone.

---

#### **SECTION D: A Fallback If The Primary Path Fails**

**If you cannot establish stable passive income of €920/month:**

**Alternative pathway:** Portugal D6 Temporary Residence Visa
**Triggers:** This applies if you have liquid savings but do not have 1+ year of documented passive income, or if passive income is irregular or difficult to verify.

**Step 1: Establish proof of liquid assets** (1–2 weeks)
- Provide recent bank statements showing €24,780 (~$25,000 USD) in savings or liquid assets.

**Step 2: Submit D6 application** (1 month)
- Same documentation as D7, except: instead of passive income proof, provide proof of savings/liquid assets.

**Step 3: Receive temporary residence card** (30–60 days after approval)
- Card is valid for 1 year and renewable annually.

**What changes from the primary path:**
- You no longer need to prove passive income; you instead prove liquid savings of €24,780.
- D6 is renewed annually; D7 leads to permanent residency after 5 years.
- After 1 year on D6, you can convert to D7 if you've established passive income.

**Additional barrier:** D6 is renewable annually but does not automatically lead to permanent residency. If your passive income does not materialize after 1 year, you must continue renewing D6 annually or switch to another pathway (e.g., employment-based visa).

**When to consider this fallback:**
- If your income is from recent investments or recent employment that doesn't yet show 1+ year of history, D6 bridges the gap.
- If you have savings but no passive income, D6 is available immediately.
- If approved for D6, you can work toward D7 conversion while already in Portugal with legal residence.

### **Example 2: "Fast But Fragile" Output**
**Scenario:** User in the U.S. facing documented persecution; can physically travel; has documentation of persecution; has legal representation; meets recognized refugee ground.

**Country/Pathway:** Canada via IRCC Refugee Status Determination + Mexico COMAR as fallback

---

#### **SECTION A: Why This Option Is Safer Than The U.S.**

Canada has federal refugee protections, comprehensive hate crime legislation, and constitutional protections for reproductive freedom.

**Definition:**
- Refugee Status Determination is based on UN refugee convention grounds: persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. Gender-based persecution (including reproductive freedom violations) is recognized as a ground under Canadian case law.
- Federal hate crime legislation explicitly covers crimes motivated by sexual orientation, gender identity, and race (Criminal Code, s. 718.2(a)(i)).
- Abortion is legal on request at all stages of pregnancy (no federal restriction).
- Charter of Rights and Freedoms provides constitutional protection for equality (s. 15) and freedom from discrimination.

**Source:** Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC); UNHCR Refugee Status Determination guidelines; Canadian Criminal Code; Canada Health Act.

**This differs from U.S. law because:** The U.S. offers no federal asylum protection based on gender or reproductive persecution. U.S. federal hate crime law does not include sexual orientation or gender identity. No federal abortion access guarantee exists (varies by state).

**Current status:** High confidence. Canadian refugee law has been stable since 1989. Reproductive persecution as a ground was reaffirmed in 2023 IRCC guidance.

---

#### **SECTION B: What Makes It Difficult**

The IRCC Refugee Status Determination pathway requires:

1. **Physical presence at a Canadian port of entry or inland IRCC office** — you must be in Canada or traveling to Canada to lodge the claim.
   *Difficulty:* If you cannot travel to Canada due to safety or legal barriers, you cannot file a refugee claim from the U.S. Offshore applications are not available through IRCC.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* You must physically appear; no virtual claims are accepted for initial filing. Once claimed, the process begins.

2. **Credible documentation of persecution** — you must provide evidence (police report, court order, medical records, affidavits, threat documentation) that demonstrates persecution or risk of persecution based on a recognized ground.
   *Difficulty:* If persecution was not formally documented (no police report, no court case, no medical records), IRCC will assess credibility based on your testimony alone. Lack of corroborating evidence significantly increases rejection risk. UNHCR estimates credibility assessment is the primary reason for refugee claim denial.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* Gathering documentation takes 1–4 weeks depending on availability. IRCC reviews for completeness during the initial interview.

3. **Procedural compliance over 10–14 months** — you must attend scheduled interviews, biometric appointments (fingerprints, photos), medical examinations, and security checks without missing deadlines.
   *Difficulty:* If you lack stable housing, reliable phone contact, or immigration legal support, missing a notice or deadline will result in automatic dismissal of your claim. IRCC sends notices via mail; if you move without updating your address, you may miss critical deadlines.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* Initial interview typically occurs within 8–12 weeks of claim. Subsequent appointments (biometrics, medical) occur over 6–10 additional weeks. Final decision comes 2–4 weeks after final interview.

4. **Immigration and Refugee Board (IRB) hearing** — if IRCC determines eligibility, your case goes to IRB for final determination.
   *Difficulty:* IRB hearings are quasi-judicial; they require presenting testimony, cross-examination, and legal argument. Without legal representation, credibility assessment relies heavily on your own presentation. Current backlog means hearings are scheduled 6–12 months after IRCC determination.
   *Documentation/Timeline:* Hearing itself takes 1–3 days. Decision issued 2–8 weeks after hearing.

**Note:** Users who answered "No" to Intake Question 1 (physical presence) cannot pursue this pathway. Users who answered "No" to Question 4 (documentation) should evaluate whether affidavits or corroborating witnesses can be gathered. Users who answered "No" to Question 7 (legal representation) face significantly higher rejection risk; pro-bono legal aid is available but often backlogged.

---

#### **SECTION C: The Fastest Viable Legal Pathway**

**Fastest pathway:** IRCC Refugee Status Determination (inland claim)
**Timeline:** Month 1 (arrival & initial claim) → Month 3–4 (IRCC initial interview) → Month 5–8 (biometrics & medical) → Month 9–12 (IRCC eligibility determination) → Month 12–20 (IRB hearing) → Months 20–22 (decision).
**Current Status:** Backlog. Canada Express Entry backlog is at 3-year high; IRCC refugee processing shows 10–14 month delays (current as of January 2026). Standard processing (non-backlog) would be 6–7 months IRCC + 4–6 months IRB.

**Step 1: Travel to Canada and lodge initial claim** (Week 1)
- Arrive at Canadian port of entry (airport, land border) or proceed to inland IRCC office (Toronto, Vancouver, Montreal, or regional office).
- Declare intention to claim refugee protection to Canadian Border Services Agency (CBSA) officer.
- Complete initial questionnaire (Schedule A).
- Receive Inland Sponsorship Letter and Undertaking (if approved for in-Canada processing).

**Step 2: Attend IRCC Initial Interview** (Weeks 8–16)
- IRCC officer conducts 2–3 hour interview covering your persecution story, documentation, and grounds for protection.
- You present written statement and documentation (police report, medical records, threat evidence).
- If represented by lawyer, lawyer attends and can present arguments.

**Step 3: Provide Biometrics & Medical Examination** (Weeks 16–24)
- IRCC schedules biometric appointment (fingerprints, photo).
- You attend panel physician medical exam (TB, HIV, vaccinations).

**Step 4: Receive IRCC Eligibility Determination** (Weeks 28–36)
- IRCC issues decision: "Referred to IRB" or "Not eligible" (if fails credibility or procedural grounds).
- If referred to IRB, you receive IRB hearing notice with date.

**Step 5: Attend IRB Hearing** (Weeks 40–60)
- Quasi-judicial hearing before Immigration and Refugee Board member.
- You testify under oath; persecution story is examined and cross-examined.
- Lawyer (if available) presents arguments.

**Step 6: Receive Final Decision** (Weeks 62–70)
- IRB issues written decision: "Convention Refugee," "Person in Need of Protection," or "Rejected."
- If approved: you become Permanent Resident with work permit and healthcare access.

**What "viable" means for this pathway:**
- You must have experienced persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, social group membership, or gender-based persecution (reproductive freedom, sexual orientation, gender identity).
- You must be able to establish credibility through testimony and documentary evidence.
- You must attend all scheduled appointments and maintain contact with IRCC/IRB.
- You must commit to residing in Canada pending determination (if rejected, you may be required to leave).

**Current challenge:** Significant. Canada's IRCC processing time is 10–14 months due to backlog (up from historical 6–7 months). This delay extends your status as a claimant (you are not yet a permanent resident). During the claim process, you receive a work permit, but your status is conditional and temporary. If rejected at any stage, you have limited appeal options and may be subject to removal proceedings.

---

#### **SECTION D: A Fallback If The Primary Path Fails**

**If your IRCC claim is rejected or you cannot travel to Canada:**

**Alternative pathway:** Mexico COMAR (Mexican Commission for Refugee Assistance) refugee determination
**Triggers:** This applies if: (1) your IRCC claim is rejected and you are subject to removal from Canada, or (2) you cannot physically reach Canada but can reach Mexico.

**Step 1: Enter Mexico and lodge initial COMAR request** (Week 1)
- Arrive in Mexico (any port of entry or border crossing).
- Go to any Mexican immigration office (Oficina de Migración) or COMAR office (Mexico City, Tapachula, or regional office).
- Complete COMAR intake form (admissibilidad) stating grounds for protection.

**Step 2: Attend COMAR Initial Interview** (Weeks 2–8)
- COMAR officer conducts interview assessing initial admissibility (does your case meet basic refugee criteria?).
- You present testimony and documentation.

**Step 3: Receive Temporary Humanitarian Status Card (if admissible)** (Weeks 4–12)
- COMAR issues temporary status allowing you to remain in Mexico while determination proceeds.
- You receive a card (cédula de refugiado temporal) valid 6 months, renewable.

**Step 4: Attend COMAR Full Determination Interview** (Weeks 12–24)
- COMAR asylum officer conducts full interview on merits of your claim.
- You present full testimony and evidence.

**Step 5: Receive Final COMAR Decision** (Weeks 26–36)
- COMAR issues decision: "Refugee" or "Not Eligible."
- If approved: you receive 1-year refugee status card, renewable, with path to permanent residency after 4 years.

**What changes from the primary path:**
- You no longer need to reach Canada; Mexico is accessible by land from the U.S.
- COMAR processing is faster than IRCC (3–6 months total vs. 10–14+ months).
- COMAR acceptance rate is higher than IRCC for some persecution grounds (Mexico recognizes broader definitions of persecution).
- If approved, Mexico offers immediate work authorization; you can begin employment during the determination process.

**Additional barrier:** Mexico's LGBTQ+ legal protections are weaker than Canada's (Mexico 3.5/5 vs. Canada 5/5). Hate crime laws exist federally but enforcement is inconsistent by state. Reproductive access is restricted in many northern states. Therefore, if your persecution is based on gender identity or reproductive freedom, Mexico may offer protection legally but enforcement and social safety are less guaranteed than Canada. However, if your primary persecution ground is political opinion, nationality, or religion, Mexico's COMAR system is viable.

**When to consider this fallback:**
- If your IRCC claim is rejected and Canada removal proceedings begin, you can apply for asylum in Mexico before removal.
- If you cannot physically reach Canada due to border restrictions or safety concerns, Mexico is an alternative entry point for protection claims.
- If processing speed is critical and you can meet Mexico's (lower) safety threshold, COMAR processes faster.
- If your persecution ground does not heavily depend on gender/sexuality, Mexico's COMAR is a workable alternative.

## Part 3: Boundary Language & Limitations

### **"What This Tool Cannot Do" — Mandatory Disclaimer**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     WHAT PASSAGR CANNOT AND DOES NOT DO                     │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

This tool provides research-based information about immigration pathways and
legal protections in 16 countries. It does NOT provide the following:

1. LEGAL ADVICE or LEGAL REPRESENTATION
   - Passagr is not a lawyer or law firm.
   - Results are informational, not legal counsel.
   - Immigration law is jurisdiction-specific and changes frequently.
   - You must consult a qualified immigration attorney licensed in the country
     of destination before making any decision or filing any application.
   - Pro-bono legal aid organizations are listed at the end of this guidance.

2. PERSONALIZED ASSESSMENT
   - Passagr cannot evaluate your specific circumstances beyond intake questions.
   - Your actual eligibility depends on details not captured by the intake
     (e.g., exact persecution documentation, consular discretion, your criminal
     history, gaps in employment, visa fraud, medical conditions).
   - A lawyer, not this tool, can assess your true eligibility.

3. GUARANTEES OF APPROVAL OR SPEED
   - Processing times in this research reflect January 2026 averages.
   - Your individual case may take longer due to: application volume,
     incomplete documentation, missing appointments, consular delays,
     policy changes, or changes in your circumstances.
   - No country guarantees approval. Rejection is possible at any stage.
   - Passagr does not guarantee you will be approved for any pathway.

4. SAFETY ASSESSMENT FOR YOUR SPECIFIC THREAT
   - Passagr describes legal protections (laws, hate crime statutes, abortion
     access).
   - It does NOT assess: whether you will actually be safe given your identity,
     your perpetrators' resources, cultural enforcement of laws, local
     corruption, or enforcement gaps.
   - Legal protection ≠ practical safety. A law can exist and be poorly enforced.
   - You must independently evaluate whether the destination country's actual
     social and enforcement environment meets your needs.
   - Local LGBTQ+ organizations, reproductive rights groups, and human rights
     monitors in destination countries can provide on-the-ground safety
     assessments.

5. MEDICAL, FINANCIAL, or SOCIAL PREPARATION
   - This tool does not provide: medical screening guidance, financial planning,
     job search support, housing assistance, language training, or mental health
     support.
   - You will likely need all of these before and after arrival.
   - Resettlement organizations and NGOs listed below offer these services.

6. UPDATES TO POLICY or LEGISLATION
   - Passagr research is current as of January 2026.
   - Immigration law changes frequently (visa requirements, processing times,
     eligibility criteria, refugee standards, abortion laws).
   - Before filing any application, verify directly with the destination
     country's official immigration ministry that requirements have not changed.
   - Do not assume dates, timelines, or eligibility based on this tool's data.

7. SOLUTIONS IF YOU ARE REJECTED
   - If an application is denied, your options depend on the specific reason
     for denial (lack of documentation, credibility concerns, policy change,
     disqualifying factors).
   - Only a lawyer can assess appeals, alternative pathways, or re-application
     strategies.
   - Passagr cannot predict or prepare you for rejection scenarios.

8. INSURANCE AGAINST RISK
   - Immigration decisions are final and carry consequences.
   - If a pathway fails, you may have limited ability to access alternative
     pathways quickly.
   - You may incur significant costs (lawyer fees, visa fees, travel, delays).
   - Passagr cannot insure you against these risks.

9. PROOF THAT ANY COUNTRY IS "SAFE"
   - Passagr documents legal protections, not actual safety.
   - No country is risk-free. Hate crimes occur in countries with strong laws.
     Abortion restrictions are enforced unevenly. Laws change.
   - You are making a choice about calculated risk, not eliminating risk.

10. ACCOMMODATION FOR EDGE CASES
    - If your situation doesn't fit the intake questions, Passagr cannot advise.
    - Examples: complex custody issues, visa fraud history, undocumented family
      members, mental health crises, disabilities requiring specific
      accommodations, trafficking survivors, previous asylum rejections.
    - A lawyer and social worker together can assess these cases.
    - Specialized NGOs (trafficking support, disability access, LGBTQ+ asylum)
      are listed below.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT YOU MUST DO INDEPENDENTLY:

Before any decision, you must:

□ Consult a qualified immigration lawyer in your origin country AND in the
  destination country you are considering.
□ Verify all legal information directly with the destination country's
  official immigration ministry website.
□ Research the destination country's actual enforcement and social environment
  (not just laws) through LGBTQ+ groups, reproductive rights organizations,
  and human rights monitors.
□ Assess your financial situation: visa fees, travel, housing, legal fees
  (often $3,000–$15,000), living expenses during processing, and contingency.
□ Plan for worst-case scenario: application rejected, you must return,
  limited pathway options remain.
□ Connect with resettlement organizations in your origin country and
  destination country for practical support.
□ Preserve documentation: all evidence of persecution, threat, medical status,
  employment, banking, and identity.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHEN TO STOP USING PASSAGR AND GET HUMAN HELP:

□ If you are in immediate danger: Contact local law enforcement or a crisis line.
□ If you cannot answer the intake questions: A lawyer or victim advocate can help
  frame your situation.
□ If you have complex circumstances (criminal history, visa fraud, custody issues,
  disabilities, trafficking): You need a lawyer, not a tool.
□ If you are rejected by one pathway: A lawyer can assess alternative options.
□ If a country's policy changes after you read this tool: Verify directly with
  that country's official ministry.
□ If you are suffering from trauma or mental health crisis: A mental health
  professional is needed, not immigration guidance.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT PASSAGR IS:

Passagr is a compendium of publicly available immigration data organized to
help you understand your options before consulting a lawyer. It is NOT a
substitute for legal advice, and it does NOT replace the judgment of a
qualified immigration professional who can assess your specific situation.
```

## Part 4: Tone Rationale

### Why Neutrality ≠ Coldness: A Trauma-Informed Framing Approach

---

#### **The Challenge**

Users of Passagr are making decisions about leaving their country of origin. They may be:
- Escaping persecution based on gender, sexuality, reproductive status, or political belief
- Fleeing active threats to their physical safety or legal status
- Under extreme time pressure (policy changes, escalating danger, legal deadlines)
- Experiencing trauma, grief, fear, and grief over displacement
- Isolated from trusted support systems
- Financially vulnerable
- Uncertain whether they will be believed or accepted

In this context, tone is not incidental—it directly affects whether users trust the information and make decisions aligned with their actual circumstances.

#### **Why We Avoid Common Fallacies**

**Problem 1: Motivational Language ("You Can Do This")**

What it does:
- Implies certainty where none exists
- Centers the speaker's confidence, not the user's actual probability of success
- Feels manipulative when the user later experiences rejection or delay

Why it's harmful in this context:
- Users need realism, not reassurance. They are already afraid.
- If a user acts on encouragement and is rejected, they experience betrayal ("this tool told me I could do it").
- Motivational language minimizes real barriers.

What we do instead:
- State barriers plainly, including their difficulty and likelihood.
- Name what determines success (lawyer quality, documentation completeness, policy timing).
- Let users assess whether they can meet the barriers.

---

**Problem 2: Minimization ("It's Just Routine")**

What it does:
- Normalizes steps that carry real consequences
- Suggests the process is easier than the research shows it is
- Erases the emotional/financial/temporal cost

Why it's harmful:
- Users plan based on unrealistic timelines and discover delays; they lose trust.
- Undocumented or vulnerable users who think "just routine" spend savings on non-refundable fees.
- Processing delays are not routine; they are current reality (January 2026). Calling them routine is a lie.

What we do instead:
- Name the current backlog and what causes delays (application volume, policy change, individual circumstance).
- State processing time as a range and explain what variables affect it.
- Acknowledge emotional and financial cost explicitly.

---

**Problem 3: Comparative Framing ("This Is Better Than That")**

What it does:
- Ranks options as if there is a universally "best" choice
- Obscures trade-offs
- Can steer users toward options that may not fit their situation

Why it's harmful:
- What is "best" depends on each user's specific barriers, timeline, resources, and risk tolerance.
- Comparisons invite users to ignore the intake questions and pick based on ranking.
- A "slower but safer" path is better for a user with documentation; a "faster but fragile" path is necessary for a user in immediate danger.

What we do instead:
- Describe each pathway's trade-offs: speed vs. stability, income vs. employment, proximity to danger.
- Name what determines viability for each option (intake answers).
- Avoid ranking. Let users choose based on their specific situation.

---

**Problem 4: False Certainty About Laws ("Portugal Will Protect You")**

What it does:
- Conflates legal protection with actual enforcement
- Implies the user will be safe if they reach the destination
- Ignores enforcement gaps, cultural conservatism, corruption, or violence outside legal channels

Why it's harmful:
- A country can have strong hate crime laws and still experience significant hate crimes.
- Abortion can be legal on paper and still be difficult to access due to provider scarcity or conscience clauses.
- LGBTQ+ rights can be protected by law and still be unsafe in practice due to cultural hostility.
- If a user reaches Portugal expecting legal protection and experiences discrimination or violence, they feel betrayed.

What we do instead:
- Describe what the law says, not what it guarantees.
- Distinguish between legal protections (codified in statute) and social safety (enforcement, cultural environment, local implementation).
- Encourage users to independently research on-the-ground conditions through local LGBTQ+, reproductive rights, and human rights organizations.
- Acknowledge that legal protection and practical safety are not the same.

---

#### **What Neutrality Actually Means**

Neutrality ≠ coldness. It means:

1. **State facts without emotional coloring.**
   - ❌ "The processing time is frustratingly long" (projects frustration)
   - ✅ "The processing time is 10–14 months, which is longer than standard due to current backlog" (factual)

2. **Acknowledge difficulty without softening it.**
   - ❌ "The documentation requirement is straightforward" (minimizes)
   - ✅ "The documentation requirement is complex. You must provide 1+ year of tax returns, which is difficult if you have irregular income or recent employment changes" (honest)

3. **Respect the user's fear and intelligence.**
   - ❌ "Don't worry, everything will be fine" (dismissive)
   - ✅ "This pathway carries real risk. If you are rejected, your legal options may be limited. A lawyer can assess whether the risk is justified in your case" (respects their autonomy)

4. **Avoid false hope and false doom.**
   - ❌ "You'll definitely get approved" (false hope)
   - ❌ "You'll definitely be rejected if you don't speak the language" (false doom)
   - ✅ "Language barriers can delay processing and complicate interviews. A lawyer and interpreter can help. Your approval depends on documentation and credibility, not language proficiency" (accurate and actionable)

5. **Provide information that supports decision-making, not decisions.**
   - ❌ "You should move to Portugal" (directive)
   - ✅ "Portugal offers X protections and requires Y documentation. To assess whether this fits your situation, consult a lawyer and research on-the-ground conditions" (informative)

---

#### **How Neutrality Serves Trauma-Informed Care**

Users in high-stakes situations need:

1. **Truthfulness, even when it's uncomfortable.** A lie told gently is still a lie. A user who acts on false information faces consequences, not comfort.

2. **Respect for their agency.** They are the experts on their own situation. Information should support their decision-making, not make the decision for them.

3. **Recognition of fear as valid.** Fear is appropriate in high-stakes decisions. Neutrality acknowledges fear without amplifying it ("Yes, this carries risk. Here is what the risk is. Here is how to reduce it.").

4. **Clarity about what they control.** Barriers are real (processing times, policies, documentation requirements). Outcomes are uncertain. But users can control: preparation quality, legal representation, documentation completeness, backup plans.

5. **Integration with professional support.** This tool supports decisions alongside lawyers, therapists, and advocates—not instead of them.

---

#### **The Tone in Practice**

Neutral, trauma-informed tone means:

- **Naming barriers:** "The pathway requires physical presence. If you cannot safely travel, this option is not available."
- **Stating timelines honestly:** "Standard processing is 6–7 months. Current backlog extends this to 10–14 months."
- **Acknowledging emotional stakes:** "This decision carries real consequences. Take the time you need to consult a lawyer before applying."
- **Validating complexity:** "Your situation includes X, Y, and Z factors. A lawyer is needed to assess your specific eligibility."
- **Respecting autonomy:** "If you decide to apply, here is what to prepare. If you decide not to, that is also valid based on your risk tolerance."

## Part 5: Verification Checklist

Before finalizing any result card output, verify all items below:

### Content Verification
- [ ] **Safety claim is specific, not vague.** (Not "Portugal is safe"; yes "Portugal has hate crime protections under Law 5/99")
- [ ] **Safety claim cites source and date.** (January 2026 currency; authoritative source)
- [ ] **Safety claim distinguishes legal protection from practical enforcement.** (Law exists and is enforced OR law exists but enforcement is inconsistent OR law is conditional)
- [ ] **Barriers are tied to intake questions.** (User can understand why they would or wouldn't face each barrier)
- [ ] **Barriers include difficulty + timeline.** (Not just "need documentation"; yes "need 1+ year tax returns, which takes 2–4 weeks to obtain from IRS")
- [ ] **Pathway timeline is realistic and includes all steps.** (Not compressed; not overstated)
- [ ] **Fallback pathway requires different preconditions.** (Not just "same path, slower version")
- [ ] **All timelines reflect January 2026 data; backlogs are named.** (Current as of [DATE], not historical)

### Tone Verification
- [ ] **No motivational language.** (No "You can do this," "Don't worry," "You'll be okay")
- [ ] **No false certainty.** (No "guaranteed," "definitely," "will be approved")
- [ ] **No minimization.** (No "just," "simply," "routine," "straightforward" applied to complex steps)
- [ ] **No comparative ranking.** (Not "better," "best," "worst," "faster is good")
- [ ] **No legal advice.** (Describes what law says; does not say what user should do)
- [ ] **Fear is respected, not amplified.** (Acknowledges real risk; does not catastrophize)
- [ ] **User agency is centered.** (Information supports their decision; doesn't make it for them)

### Language Verification
- [ ] **Jargon is minimal.** (Technical terms explained or linked to definitions)
- [ ] **Sentences are active and direct.** (Subject-verb-object; not passive or vague)
- [ ] **Numbers are specific.** (Not "affordable income"; yes "€920/month (~$950 USD)")
- [ ] **Conditions are named.** (Not "if you qualify"; yes "if you have 1+ year of tax returns")
- [ ] **Assumptions are questioned.** (If pathway assumes X, state that assumption)

### Completeness Verification
- [ ] **All four sections present.** (Why safer, What's difficult, Fastest pathway, Fallback)
- [ ] **Intake question language used.** (User can connect result to their answers)
- [ ] **Next steps are clear.** ("Consult a lawyer"; "Verify with official ministry"; "Research on-the-ground conditions")
- [ ] **Pro-bono/free resources listed.** (Legal aid orgs, NGOs, resettlement support)
